# Book Cover Design Brief
## "What is Love? — The Law of the Gospel: Love Perfected in Christ"

**Author:** Patrick Michael Steil
**Publisher:** First Edition 2026
**Audience:** Catholics, OCIA candidates, Christian families, youth ministers

---

## Vision & Tone

This is not a self-help book. It is not contemporary or trendy. It is a sacred text — a faithful transmission of 2,000 years of Catholic wisdom on the nature of love as revealed by God through Scripture, the Catechism, and the saints. The cover must communicate this with gravitas.

**Aesthetic anchors:**
- Think illuminated manuscript meets classical Catholic sacred art
- Muted, deep, timeless — not bright, flat, or modern
- The book should feel like it belongs beside Scripture on someone's nightstand
- Quiet reverence, not triumphalism or marketing polish

---

## Typography

**Title:** *What is Love?*
- Display serif — classical, weighty, authoritative
- Not decorative script; think something close to Trajan, IM Fell English, or Cormorant Garamond at large size
- The question mark is intentional and important — retain it

**Subtitle:** *The Law of the Gospel: Love Perfected in Christ*
- Smaller, refined serif — set below the title with breathing room
- Slightly lighter weight; subordinate but legible

**Author name:** Intentionally omitted from the front cover and spine. The author sees himself as a transmitter of received truth, not its originator. No byline anywhere on the exterior. Author credit appears only on the interior copyright page.

**Tagline (spine or back):**
> *"God's Perfect Love is the sacred gift of covenant which binds truth, justice, mercy and sacrifice into life-giving communion."*

---

## Central Imagery

The cover should carry a single, strong visual metaphor. Choose **one** of the following directions and develop it with restraint:

### Option A — The Three-Tissue Sacred Heart, Bound by Thorns, Wrapped Around the Cross
The Sacred Heart rendered with three visually distinct layers woven into the heart muscle itself — each representing one pillar of love. The cross rises from the center; the heart wraps around it. The crown of thorns encircles the heart from the outside, binding the three tissues together.

**The theological structure of the image:**
- **Cross** = Sacrifice (the axis, the act — not a layer but the center around which everything is organized)
- **Heart tissue, three layers** = Truth, Justice, Mercy (the substance of love - integrated and inseparable from one another)
- **Crown of thorns** = the Binding — the covenant that holds Truth, Justice, and Mercy together; suffering willingly embraced as the bond of love

The three layers are not labels or diagram sections — they are organic, interwoven, inseparable. Think braided rope strands, or the visible grain of carved wood, or the layered strata of ancient stone. Each layer carries its tradition color:
- **Truth** — Gold / luminous white (the innermost layer, closest to the cross — the Word at the center)
- **Justice** — Deep violet / purple (strong, structural, the weight-bearing middle layer)
- **Mercy** — Pale rose-white (the outermost layer, soft and enveloping)

The crown of thorns is a separate element — it wraps the outside of the heart, distinct from the three tissue layers. It is not a symbol of anguish here but of covenant bond: the voluntary, sacrificial, willing embrace that holds everything together.

No flames (avoid sentimentality). The heart is strong and luminous, not anguished. Render in the manner of Byzantine sacred art or a medieval woodcut — aged line work, not photorealistic. Dark ground. Gold accents at the cross.

The cover should feel as though the viewer is looking at something ancient that was always true, and is only now being clearly seen.

### Option B — The Sacred Heart / Cross
A stylized Sacred Heart or Latin cross rendered in the manner of a medieval woodcut or Byzantine icon. No photorealism. Aged line work, perhaps golden or deep red on dark parchment or deep blue-black. The cross as covenant, sacrifice, and life.

### Option C — The Four Rivers from the Cross
Four rivers flow outward from the base of a Latin cross — each representing one pillar of God's Perfect Love. They should feel like water, light, or living vine rather than labeled arrows. The Cross is the singular source; the rivers are its fruit flowing into the world.

Color of each river drawn from Catholic tradition:
- **Truth** — Gold / warm white (Byzantine iconographic gold = divine uncreated light; white = the Word made visible, the Transfiguration)
- **Justice** — Deep violet / purple (sovereign authority, God's law; liturgical color of Lent and divine judgment)
- **Mercy** — Pale white-rose (the pale ray of the Divine Mercy image — St. Faustina's approved vision — water flowing from Christ's wounded side, purification and new life)
- **Sacrifice** — Deep crimson / scarlet (the red ray of the Divine Mercy image; Passion vestments, martyrs, Good Friday; "without the shedding of blood there is no forgiveness," Heb 9:22)

Note: Mercy and Sacrifice both draw on red but must read as distinct — sacrifice is deep crimson (blood poured out in death), mercy is the pale rose-white (blood as gift of living water). The Divine Mercy image encodes exactly this distinction from the same wound.

Render in the style of an illuminated manuscript or medieval mosaic. Dark ground (navy or charcoal), four rivers luminous against it. The cross itself in aged gold, unadorned.

### Option D — The Covenant Knot
An interlaced Celtic or medieval knotwork symbol — representing the binding nature of love's covenant (truth, justice, mercy, sacrifice woven together). The four strands are visually distinct using the same four tradition colors: gold (truth), violet (justice), pale rose-white (mercy), deep crimson (sacrifice). They braid together inseparably — no strand can be removed without the knot collapsing. Rendered on deep forest green or dark wine ground. Simple, geometric, ancient-feeling.

### Option E — Chalice and Scripture
A chalice and an open book (Gospel) rendered in the style of a miniature illumination — gold leaf details, jewel-toned accents, richly bordered. References both the Eucharist (life-giving communion) and Sacred Scripture (truth).

**Optional detail for the designer's discernment:** The Cross and the altar are theologically one — the Mass re-presents the one sacrifice of Calvary on the altar (CCC 1382–1383). If any imagery option presents the Cross and there is a natural, unforced way to suggest the Cross *as* altar — through the geometry of its base, the presence of candles, a stone surface, a hint of altar linen (corporale), or any other liturgically resonant hint — that would add a meaningful layer. This is entirely optional and should only be done if it arises organically from the chosen direction. Do not force it.

## Color Palette

Choose from these tonalities — do not combine all; pick one primary palette:

- **Dark Wine / Aged Parchment / Dull Gold** — covenant blood, ancient manuscript, scriptural warmth
- **Forest Green / Gold / Cream** — life, growth, classical devotional book
- **Charcoal / Warm White / Crimson** — gravity, clarity, the Cross
- **Deep Navy / Gold / Ivory** — authority, wisdom, sacred night sky

Avoid: bright or saturated colors, pastels, gradients that feel digital or "tech", stock-photo aesthetics.

---

## What to Avoid

- Stock photography of any kind
- Photorealistic human figures (faces, hands)
- Modern minimalist sans-serif typography
- Abstract or secular contemporary art styles
- Anything that could be mistaken for a self-help or wellness book
- Anything that looks like a greeting card

---

## Reference Aesthetic Touchstones

- The design of the *Ignatius Catholic Study Bible* or *Ignatius Press* trade editions
- The Douay-Rheims Bible (old bindings, gilt lettering)
- Classic Loeb Classical Library design language — restrained, scholarly
- The cover aesthetic of Dietrich von Hildebrand's *The Heart* or John Paul II's *Theology of the Body* (various Pauline Books editions)
- Medieval Book of Hours illuminated borders — as inspiration for decorative framing only

## Spirit of the Book (for the designer's context)

The author writes: *"I collected all the words and strung them together. But I cannot claim to be the author."* He sees himself as a transmitter of divine truth received through Scripture, the Catechism, the saints, and the Church's 2,000-year tradition.

The core definition of the book:
> **"God's Perfect Love is the sacred gift of covenant — binding truth, justice, mercy and sacrifice — into life-giving communion."**

This book is ultimately written *for children* — for the next generation who deserve to know what love truly is. It is urgent, but it is not anxious. It is ancient truth offered with pastoral warmth.

The cover should feel like you are holding something precious. Like truth that has been guarded and handed down. Not a product. A gift.

## Back Cover Copy

**Top line (large, centered — the question that opens everything):**
*"God is love, and he who abides in love abides in God, and God abides in him."*
— 1 John 4:16

---

**Body text (~110 words):**

Every ideology, every movement, every broken relationship is searching for the same thing: truth, justice, mercy, love. Separated from one another, they become counterfeits — and counterfeits create the very evils they claim to solve. What the world needs is a definition of love precise enough to tell the real from the false.

This book is the culmination of a 37-year journey to understand and answer that question with clarity and conviction. Drawing on Sacred Scripture, the Catechism, and 2,000 years of Catholic wisdom, it offers a precise, biblically and theologically grounded definition of God's Perfect Love — and shows how that definition unlocks the meaning of salvation, the nature of sin, the purpose of the sacraments, and the fullness of the Gospel.

*God's Perfect Love is the sacred gift of covenant —
binding truth, justice, mercy and sacrifice —
into life-giving communion.*

Not a new idea. The ancient truth, illuminated.

---

**Bottom line (small, above barcode):**
For parish, ministry, and personal use: https://whatislove.life

---

**Designer note on back cover layout:**
- Title (*What is Love?*) repeated in small caps or restrained serif at the very top
- Scripture quote in italic, centered, modest size
- Body text in readable serif, ~10–11pt
- Definition set apart — perhaps slightly indented or in a lighter weight, as a standalone stanza
- Final line in small caps or reduced size
- Barcode and ISBN bottom-right, with breathing room above it
- Keep the same dark ground and color palette as the front — the book should feel unified front-to-back
