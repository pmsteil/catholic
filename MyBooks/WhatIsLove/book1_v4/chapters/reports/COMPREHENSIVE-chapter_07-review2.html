🟡 RECOMMENDED Lines 25-47 (The Universal Cry section)
The section makes the point about needing a transcendent standard effectively but then belabors it with a long bulleted list of what justice 'cannot be.' The argument is won by line 35; the enumeration afterward slows momentum.
Original: The moment you recognize this, you're appealing to an objective standard of justice—one that cannot be human opinion (humans disagree), cultural convention (cultures differ), evolutionary advantage (evolution cannot explain moral *authority*), majority vote (majorities can be wrong), or personal feeling (feelings conflict).
Suggested: The moment you recognize this, you're appealing to an objective standard—one that transcends human opinion, cultural convention, majority vote, or personal feeling. All of these shift and conflict; justice must be grounded in something unchanging.
🟡 RECOMMENDED Lines 119-145 (Justice Grounded in Imago Dei section)
The discussion of embodiment and body-theology (John Paul II's Theology of the Body references) is valuable but feels somewhat inserted. The transition from 'rights are ordered toward the good' to 'we're embodied persons' is abrupt. A bridging sentence would help.
Suggested: Add a transition such as: 'And this dignity we possess as image-bearers is not merely spiritual—it is embodied. We are not souls trapped in bodies; we are body-soul unities.'
🟡 RECOMMENDED Lines 177-210 (Justice vs. Partiality section)
This section is important but risks alienating readers who see legitimate concerns in DEI frameworks. The chapter correctly distinguishes biblical concern for the vulnerable from ideological partiality, but could strengthen this by briefly acknowledging that some real injustices (e.g., historical racism) exist that require structural redress—while still critiquing how modern frameworks go wrong. Without this, the section may read as dismissive of genuine grievances.
Suggested: Add one sentence acknowledging real historical injustices before the critique: 'Real injustices—slavery, Jim Crow, systemic discrimination—have existed and their effects persist. The question is whether modern ideological frameworks address them justly or create new forms of partiality.'
🟡 RECOMMENDED Lines 213-230 (Justice in Different Relationships section)
The section on 'Justice Toward Enemies' is brief (one sentence). Given the book's target audience and the cultural moment, this could be expanded to address how justice toward enemies relates to mercy (avoiding revenge while maintaining accountability). This would also strengthen the bridge to the mercy chapter.
Suggested: Expand to 2-3 sentences: 'Justice Toward Enemies: Even enemies deserve basic human dignity. Justice means not dehumanizing them, not seeking revenge, not violating their fundamental rights. But justice toward enemies also points us toward mercy—recognizing that the enemy, too, bears God's image and may yet be transformed.'
