1. Canonical Definitions & Standing Rules

Why This Book Exists — The Dictionary Problem

A major premise of the entire book — and the reason it is called "What Is Love?" — is that the deeper Patrick studies apologetics, the more he sees that Christians disagree not because they disagree on Scripture, but because they disagree on what words mean. Catholics and Protestants are often reading the same verses but carrying different dictionaries. The most important word to define is love. Once that definition is settled — with its four pillars intact — most of the other disagreements (faith/works, grace/merit, sacraments, authority) begin to fall into place. The book is, at bottom, a dictionary entry for the most important word in the Christian vocabulary.

The Canonical Definition of Love

"God's Perfect Love is the sacred gift of covenant which binds truth, justice, mercy, and sacrifice into life-giving communion."

Word-for-word, no variation. Note: without "sacrificial" — Patrick's deliberate choice. The four pillars (Truth, Justice, Mercy, Sacrifice) are co-constitutive. No pillar is metaphysically prior to any other.

The Aquinas Integration (Patrick's Key Insight)

"In Augustine's definition of love being willing the good of the other, I think the good in that definition is the truth, justice and mercy. And the willing it for the other is the sacrifice part."

amor est velle alicui bonum — the good (bonum) = Truth + Justice + Mercy; willing it for another (velle alicui) = Sacrifice. The four pillars are not four additions to the definition — they are its interior anatomy.

Cross-test: Calvary = Aquinas at full volume. Bonum = truth about our condition + justice satisfied + mercy poured; velle alicui = "for us men and for our salvation" at the cost of everything.

Standing Rules (Never Violate)

  • RSV translation required for all Scripture quotations
  • Matt 28 must always include v.18 (Christ's authority) — never just 28:19-20
  • Never use: performed, earned, transaction, payment plan, checklist, debt-payment, unmercy (for sacrifice/faith context)
  • Never name Protestant traditions directly — always positive Catholic framing, never defensive or polemical
  • Pillar interplay goes in Binding Check only — don't cross-pollinate pillar arguments in body sections
  • OT sacrifices "spoke truly of" fulfillment — never "merely foreshadowed" or diminished
  • "Sacrifice completes" is acceptable; "sacrifice carries the others" is NOT (implies priority)
  • No Oxford comma in the canonical pillar list: "truth, justice, mercy and sacrifice"
  • Prefer prose over bullets — convert bullet lists to flowing narrative paragraphs
  • Gender-neutral language where applicable (e.g., "the person" not "he" for generic references)
  • Each example must be placed under the correct pillar — a pundit's hypocrisy is truth-without-sacrifice, not truth-without-justice; use the framework precisely

2. Patrick's Voice & Style

How Patrick Writes & Edits

  • Concrete over abstract — when a proposed phrase is too conceptual, he'll say it outright: "just a whole bunch of words that don't have much meaning."
  • Favors parallel grammatical structures — tracks asymmetries closely (notices "two clauses with 'giving,' two without").
  • Thinks theologically by talking through it — long voice-dictated paragraphs reveal his reasoning; then asks the AI to capture and compress what he just said.
  • Things said once, in the right place — frequently asks "is this already covered elsewhere?" before adding content.
  • Steelmans opponents without naming them — repeatedly insists on positive Catholic framing without polemical posture.
  • Tight syllogisms over extended arguments — prefers a sharp 3-sentence sequence over a paragraph of explanation.
  • Uses "vibe" / "feel" language first to describe what he wants a passage to convey emotionally, then gets into content specifics.
  • Short approvals for drafts — "y", "b", "option a" — reviews entire proposed drafts, then simply confirms or adjusts.
  • Catches his own imprecision — "But wait, is that example truth without justice?" — will push back on his own edits when the theology drifts.
  • "I'm not asking you to add these exact words... I just want the theology to be deeply felt."

Signature Phrases & Motifs Patrick Returns To

  1. "The open hand / closed fist" — central controlling metaphor for sacrifice; can be extended, even temporarily broken, but must return to it
  2. "Our love is never oriented inward. It's always oriented outward." — personal conviction from his conversion
  3. "We sacrifice because He first sacrificed" — echo of 1 John 4:19 grammar
  4. "Faith leads to love. Love grows faith. Round and round." — spiral image of sanctification
  5. "A future filled with good Catholics" — his missional vision
  6. "His perfect love is what saves the world" — chapter-close thesis phrase
  7. "To love is to be someone who sacrificially wills the truth, justice, and mercy of God for another" — working definition, recited with reader at key moments
  8. "The only difference is the preposition" — refers to the theological distinction "not alongside but into" (our sacrifice united into Christ's one sacrifice, not running parallel to it); the italicized preposition carries the whole theological weight
  9. "As we forgive" — Our Father clause, returned to repeatedly as the practical binding of mercy and sacrifice
  10. Augustine's restless heart — Confessions 1.1.1, reached for multiple times
  11. Irenaeus "man fully alive" — Against Heresies 4.20.7, brought up unprompted twice

3. Theological Positions Settled

On Faith and Works (The Positive Case)

  • Gal 5:6 as central anchor: "faith working through love" — not faith plus love, not faith and then love — one organic reality
  • "Faith working through love is faith sacrificing for the other" — Patrick's own formulation
  • Phil 2:12-13 as synthesis verse: "work out your own salvation... for God is at work in you" — we work out because God is at work
  • The spiral: "Faith leads us to love. Love grows us in faith. Round and round — not as a treadmill, but as the very spiral of life itself"
  • Never defensive against Protestant readings — never set up and rebut; simply state the fullness
  • Steelman the Protestant concern: "they are guarding something precious — that what saves us is what Christ did, not what we do"
  • But: rejected "finished work" language — "I don't want to give ground on his finished work because I think they have a completely wrong interpretation of that"
  • Revised steelman: "our hope rests on His Cross, not on our offerings"

James 2 — Patrick's Outward-Facing Reading

"I've been thinking about these verses for years... every Protestant argues this from the standpoint of what this verse is saying about me and my salvation. But what James is pointing out here is what I have been saying since I came back to the Catholic faith, that our love is never oriented inward. It's always oriented outward."

The question is not "am I really saved?" — it is "did anyone get fed? The brother is still hungry."

James 2:14-17 unpacked as a four-pillars X-ray: "Be warmed and filled" without action fails on all four at once — not seeing (truth), not giving what's owed (justice), heart unmoved (mercy), no cost paid (sacrifice).

On Sacrifice: Co-constitutive, Not Primary

  • "No thread is metaphysically prior" — AGENTS.md §4 rule, repeatedly honored
  • Found and fixed: "carries the other three into actual effect" → "makes truth, justice, and mercy actual rather than admired from a safe distance"
  • The closed-fist posture is the prior reality from which none of the four pillars can operate — neutral on will-vs-intellect debate (lets Thomists and Augustinians both read correctly)
  • "We do not sacrifice alongside His one sacrifice but into it" — the preposition into is load-bearing

On Divine Mercy / Universal Offer

  • The offer is universal (no sin beyond reach, CCC 605)
  • Reception is free (the free "yes" preserved)
  • NOT universalism — explicitly: "for the soul who freely turns and asks for it"
  • God will never abandon us; but love, to be love, leaves us free to abandon Him
  • Three outcomes when mercy is extended: acceptance, rejection, or enablement (mercy that avoids accountability is not mercy)
  • Assurance is real — we can have assurance that God will always hold up His end; we trust Him. We just don't trust ourselves to hold up ours. "The assurance of a beloved who stays, not the guarantee of a contract I can't break."

On Incurvatus in Se & the Closed Fist

Augustine's term for the soul curved in on itself. Patrick linked it directly to the closed-fist metaphor: "The closed fist is incurvatus in se made visible." Sacrifice liberates from this prison of self.

The seven deadly sins analysis: NOT primarily a refusal of sacrifice (Patrick checked this). The right framing is the closed-fist posture as the prior reality from which none of the four pillars can operate.

On the Book's Hidden Thesis

"Only a church that finds its origin in God as love does, and is willing to look at the fullness of truth in all of God's revelation as love does, and is willing to define what justice requires... is the church that has actually preserved the fullness of love that Christ has taught."

Submission to the Magisterium = submission to God's love (not to a human institution). No sin is ever merely personal — every sin is ecclesial and cosmic.

5. Chapter 6 (Truth) Decisions

CH 6 Natural Law Framing

Truth chapter establishes natural law — the order of creation implies an Orderer. The "two books" framing (book of nature, book of Scripture) was rejected as too neat. Instead: order-implies-Orderer argument.

CH 6 "Word of God" Is a Person

Established distinction: the Word of God is Christ Himself (John 1:1-14), not primarily the Bible. Scripture is the written witness to the Word. This shapes how the chapter frames Scripture, Tradition, and Magisterium.

CH 6 El Emet = "God of Truth" (Not "The Truth")

Hebrew precision: El Emet in Deut 32:4 means "God of Truth" — the genitive construction matters. God doesn't just tell the truth; He IS truth. Added Deut 32:4 to the God-is-Truth scriptural chain.

CH 6 Canon History (Honest Version)

The canon was "received and ratified" by the Church, not "discerned" — this is the language of the CCC and the Church's own self-understanding (the Church did not create the canon; she received it). The Mass is older than the compiled Gospels — the liturgy provided the hermeneutical key, not the other way around.

CH 6 Yields Idolatry to Ch. 7

Ch. 6 names idolatry as the downstream fruit of severing love from truth, but the full structural unpacking (Decalogue exposition, CCC 2113, modern-idols inventory) migrates to Ch. 7 where Aquinas classifies the virtue of religion under justice (ST II-II q.81). Ch. 6 keeps the mechanism sentence: love detached from truth re-attaches to a substitute.

CH 6 Counterfeit-Love Bullets → Idolatry Bridge

Four counterfeits kept: "Love means accepting me as I am," "Love never judges," "Love means letting people define their own reality," "Love means inclusion and equality at all costs." The move: these share a single root — idolatry. Love is always directed at something; sever truth and love re-attaches to a substitute.

CH 6 Pundit Example Placement

The pundit who reports truth selectively for ratings/advertisers = truth-without-justice (withholding from the public what they are owed). The pundit embroiled in adultery = truth-without-sacrifice (proclaiming truth you won't bleed for). Patrick caught the misplacement himself and corrected it.

CH 6 Kingdom Capstone + Holy Spirit Closing

Matt 6:10 ("Thy kingdom come, thy will be done") added as capstone. Chapter closes with Holy Spirit / Matt 28:20 ("I am with you always") — connecting Christ's promise to the ongoing work of truth in the Church.

CH 6 Col 1:15 Insertion

"He is the image of the invisible God" — added to the Truth chapter's Christological foundation.

6. Chapter 7 (Justice) Decisions

Justice is uniquely bi-directional. Toward God: idolatry (withholding owed worship). Toward neighbor: exploitation of the vulnerable (withholding owed dignity). Ch. 7 is the deliberate exception to the one-opposing-sin rule.

CH 7 "When the Thread Is Cut" — Two Sub-Callouts

Sub-callout A — Justice toward God severed (5 bullets): Idolatry, Sacrilege, Functional atheism, Syncretism, Presumption — each with inline Church citations.

Sub-callout B — Justice toward neighbor severed (3 bullets): Abandonment of the vulnerable, Utilitarian reduction, Dehumanization — each with inline Church citations.

CH 7 Sacrament of Reconciliation → Preview Only

Full treatment moved to Ch. 8 (Mercy is Confession's natural home). Ch. 7 keeps only a single-paragraph preview: "Justice and mercy meet sacramentally in the Sacrament of Reconciliation — we will see its full shape in the next chapter."

CH 7 "Do You Want True Justice?" — Tightened to Half

Four sequential sections felt like endings. The altar-call section kept but cut from ~20+ lines to ~8-12. Preserved: opening "Do you want human justice or God's justice?" framing; one paragraph on how God's justice differs; closing altar-call question.

CH 7 Closing Structure: Accept → Submit → Learn → Champion

Chapter closing moves through this four-step sequence — Patrick's deliberate progression for the reader's response to justice.

CH 7 Catholic Social Teaching Paragraph Added

One paragraph naming concrete applications: Rerum Novarum, Pacem in Terris, Evangelium Vitae, just war, capital punishment, immigration, just wage, care for creation. With humility note: the layman should read the encyclicals first.

Isaiah 5:20-21 anchor: calling evil good and good evil. The chapter addresses how political/cultural forces co-opt the word "justice" for their own ends. Patrick wanted this addressed directly.

CH 7 Apologetics: Bigotry Accusation

Patrick wanted the chapter to preemptively address the accusation that Catholic moral teaching = bigotry. Approach: never defensive; simply show that justice requires truth, and rendering what is owed to a person includes telling them the truth about what is good for them.

The Undercurrent of the Whole Book (Author's Deeper Purpose)

Every chapter should be read and evaluated against this underlying argument. It is not preached overtly at the reader, but it is the thread that ties every chapter together. Read a sentence, claim, or argument in any single chapter only in light of the accumulated context the reader carries from earlier chapters.

  1. Love has objective structure — it is not a feeling, slogan, or sentiment. The four pillars are the anatomy of love. Modern slogans like "love is love" are reductions of love into sentimental mercy alone, stripped of truth and justice.
  2. Sin has objective structure too — and it mirrors love's structure. Every sin attacks one or more pillars.
  3. No sin is ever "personal" — every sin is ecclesial and cosmic. Every sin wounds the Body of Christ. This undercuts the assumption that "private" sins are their own business.
  4. Christian division is itself a sin against truth. "Catholic, Protestant, Orthodox — doesn't really matter" is not humility. It is a sin against truth (Eph 4:5), justice to God, and Christ's own prayer (John 17:21). A divided Church preaches a divisible Christ; a divisible truth is the world's favorite lie.
  5. Faith alone is insufficient unless we ask: faith in what, and for what? A "faith" that does not produce truth-telling, justice-doing, mercy-showing, sacrifice-making disciples is faith in a feeling about Christ. Paul and James both point at faith working through love (Gal 5:6).
  6. Virtue is not Pelagian — it is the shape of our response to being loved first. We become virtuous because we have already been loved first. The virtues are the shape of love alive in us, not tickets. Matthew 5–7 and Philippians 3 call us to lifelong movement toward perfection.
  7. Submitting to the Magisterium is submitting to Christ's love — not to a human institution, but to Christ's own teaching authority, preserved across 2,000 years. "The gates of hell shall not prevail against her" (Matt 16:18).
  8. The Catholic Church alone holds all four pillars bound across time. The book's quiet conclusion, never argued bluntly but structurally implied. Only a Church with Magisterium, Sacred Tradition, Scripture, and the sacraments working together can hold the whole deposit. Submitting fully to the Church's teaching makes all of Scripture most true, most cohesive, and most comprehensive.
  9. The pastoral tone throughout. The book hands the reader a framework sharp enough that they see for themselves. The invitation is "look at love clearly, and follow where the fullness actually lives," and voluntarily, humbly, submit to Her.

Summary in one line: The book is a devotional on love that quietly makes the case that only the Catholic Church preserves the whole shape of Perfect Love — truth, justice, mercy, and sacrifice bound together in covenant — and that every sin against any pillar, however small, is a participation in the world's disintegration of love and a wound on the Body of Christ.

The Four-Part Pattern for Chapters 6–9

Chs. 6–9 each treat one pillar. They share a consistent four-part structure (A–D). Ch. 6 is the reference implementation.

Part A — Open by naming the sin directly opposite to that pillar. After the opening prose, insert a callout naming the opposing sin, grounded in Scripture. Terminology note: avoid "cardinal sin." Use: (1) "the sin directly opposite to X" (default), (2) "the opposing sin against X," (3) "the contrary sin against X" (Thomistic), or (4) "the antithesis of X" (most formal). Never use cardinal sin, capital sin, or mortal sin in this diagnostic sense.

Framing note for Ch. 7 (Justice): Justice is the one pillar whose opposing sin divides by direction. Toward God: idolatry. Toward neighbor: exploitation of the vulnerable. This is the deliberate exception to the one-opposing-sin rule.

Part B — The body proves why this sin destroys love, exploring the pillar positively and negatively.

Part C — Close with the end-state diagnostic under ## When the Thread Is Cut, placed after ## Binding Check and before ## The Bridge. Callback sentence naming the opposing sin again; make clear the list is illustrative, not exhaustive. End-state callout with three bullets:

  • Truth removed → manipulation / relativism / idolatry
  • Justice removed → two sub-callouts: God-ward (idolatry / sacrilege / functional atheism / syncretism / presumption) and neighbor-ward (abandonment / utilitarian reduction / dehumanization)
  • Mercy removed → cruelty / vengeance / despair
  • Sacrifice removed → transaction / barrenness / betrayal

Part D — The Bridge must explicitly name the next chapter and preview its theme. Not vague — say "In Chapter 7, we turn to justice…"

Crucial framing note for Ch. 6 (Truth): Ch. 6's opening question is an epistemological/phenomenological claim, not a metaphysical priority claim. Ch. 5 already settled that no thread is metaphysically prior. Truth is the shared existential floor every human being stands on before theology begins.

Audience

  • Primary: Everyday Catholics seeking to deepen authentic love of God and neighbor
  • Secondary: Lukewarm/cultural Catholics drifting toward easy-believism; Protestants raised in tradition-by-default; Orthodox, secular, and spiritually curious readers

Working assumption about non-Catholic readers: a reader who picked this book up is not vehemently opposed to Catholic authority. They are most likely someone whose current tradition is inherited rather than chosen, who holds sola scriptura (or none at all) as a default. Write for the open reader; the hostile reader would never have reached page one.

Tone Rules

  • Unapologetically Catholic. Use Catholic doctrine, CCC, encyclicals, Fathers, councils, Sacred Tradition, Magisterium, Natural Law freely and confidently, without qualification.
  • Confident, not combative. Introduce Catholic concepts positively and naturally. Do not hedge or soften.
  • Never defensive. Do not pre-emptively argue against sola scriptura or justify every Catholic citation. State the fullness; let it speak.
  • Never antagonistic. Do not attack Protestantism, its figures, or its principles directly. Beauty converts; argument alone rarely does.
  • Never name Protestant traditions directly. Always steelman the concern they are guarding.
  • Pastoral and invitational. Hand the reader a framework sharp enough to see for themselves where love has been reduced, and where the fullness has been preserved.
  • Personal, direct, relatable, evangelistic. Use "bite-sized theology" — substantial but accessible.

Minor note: When a non-Catholic source happens to be right (e.g., Wesley's "moving on toward perfection"), use the concept but avoid quoting them by name. Prefer Catholic sources for the same point.

The Free Will Principle (Foundational to Every Chapter)

God's love is perfect but never coerced. Free will is a gift of God, not a possession independent of God (Phil 2:13). The same freedom that empowers love empowers refusal — there is no third option. The whole Christian life is the moment-by-moment, voluntary, willing return of that gift to its Giver.

Necessary, not causal: Our free yes is necessary but not the cause of salvation. God is sole efficient cause; our consent is the causa sine qua non — the door, not the engine. Council of Trent Session VI; Augustine: "He who created you without you will not save you without you."

Three-voice pattern of free consent: Mary's fiat (Luke 1:38), Christ in Gethsemane (Luke 22:42), the Our Father (Matt 6:10). The same prayer in three voices: a creaturely will returning itself to the One who gave it.

Editing rules: Never write grace acting on a passive recipient. Never write cooperation adding anything to God. Name sin as free choice, virtue as free return. The phrase "freely, willingly, voluntarily" is the book's signal for this principle.

The Shema as Scripture's Hermeneutical Key

Christ names the Shema (Deut 6:4-5) joined to love of neighbor (Lev 19:18) as the interpretive key for all of Scripture: "On these two commandments depend all the law and the prophets" (Matt 22:40).

Three Shema passages: Deut 6:4-5 (source text), Mark 12:29-31/Matt 22:34-40 (hermeneutical principle), Luke 10:25-28 (soteriological condition — "do this, and you will live"). Luke 10 is non-negotiable wherever the book discusses faith, salvation, or faith-and-works.

Hebrew shama ("hear/listen") = etymological root of biblical obedience. Obedience is irreducibly relational — one listens to a person, not a rulebook.

Honoring the Old Covenant

Fulfillment is not replacement. OT worship was not empty rehearsal — the Israelite who laid his hand on the lamb was truly meeting God. Preferred verbs: fulfills, completes, perfects, gathers, brings to fullness. Avoid: "could only," "in pieces," "gesture toward," "shadows that..." Never diminish Israel's worship to a placeholder.

Pastoral test: would a devout Jewish reader recognize that this book honors the faith of his fathers? The Cross does not say the Temple was wrong; the Cross says the Temple was right, and reveals the One the Temple was always for.

Plain Language Rule (Book-Wide)

Write for the nominal Catholic, not the seminarian. Explain every term of art at first use, in plain English, inside the sentence. Prefer concrete over abstract — show the idea before naming it. Latin is seasoning, not the meal. Test: would a Christmas-and-Easter Catholic understand this on first read?

Protestant Engagement Rules

Patrick's Catholic Salvation Sequence

He loves us first → we come to trust Him in faith → in that trust we learn His ways of love → and we obediently live them out in sacrificial love.

No boasting, no earning. Every step is grace. But precisely because it's a living relationship: "Every single day I can choose to stop listening."
